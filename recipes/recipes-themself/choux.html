Choux Paste Recipe

Choux pastry (or pâte à choux) is a light, golden-brown pastry dough famous for its unique ability to puff up dramatically in the oven without the help of chemical leavening agents like baking powder or yeast. Instead, it relies entirely on high moisture content; as the dough bakes, the water and eggs trap steam, causing it to swell and hollow out, leaving a crisp outer shell with a light, airy interior. This versatile paste starts on the stovetop, where water, butter, and flour are cooked together into a paste before eggs are beaten in to create a glossy, pipeable consistency. Once baked, these hollow shells serve as the structural foundation for iconic desserts like cream puffs, éclairs, and profiteroles, or savory treats like gougères.

Ingredients

  • Water or Milk: Provides moisture.
  • Butter: Adds richness and fats.
  • All-Purpose Flour: Provides structure.
  • Eggs: Provides the strcutrue building protiens.

Steps

  1. Melt and boil
    2. 5 mins

    Combine your liquid (water or milk), cubed butter, and a pinch of salt/sugar in a saucepan over medium heat. Bring it to a rolling boil. The butter must be completely melted by the time the liquid boils so too much water doesn't evaporate.

  2. Dump and cook the panade
    3. 2-3 mins

    Remove the pan from the heat and dump in all the flour at once. Stir vigorously with a wooden spoon until the flour completely absorbs the liquid. Return to medium heat and cook, stirring constantly, for 1 to 2 minutes. You are looking for a smooth ball of dough that pulls away from the sides and leaves a thin film on the bottom of the pan.

  3. Cool the paste
    4. 5 mins

    Transfer the hot dough to a mixing bowl and let it cool down for about 5 minutes, stirring occasionally. It needs to drop below 140°F (60°C) before the next step. If it is too hot, it will scramble the eggs.

  4. Incorporate the eggs
    5. 5 mins

    Whisk your eggs in a separate bowl. Add them to the cooled dough one splash at a time, mixing thoroughly after each addition. At first, the dough will separate and look curdled, but keep mixing and it will come together. Stop adding egg when the dough becomes smooth, glossy, and forms a "V" shape when lifting the spoon.

  5. Pipe and bake
    6. 30-35 mins

    Transfer to a piping bag and shape your pastries on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Bake at a high heat—around 400°F (200°C)—for the first 15 minutes to generate steam and force the rise. Then, lower the heat to 350°F (180°C) for another 15-20 minutes to dry out and firm up the shells.
